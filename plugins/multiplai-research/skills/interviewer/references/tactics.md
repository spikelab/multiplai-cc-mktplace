# Advanced Interviewer Tactics

Load this file when core techniques (TEDW, follow-ups, question architecture) aren't breaking through—when the person seems guarded, defensive, or stuck in rehearsed answers.

---

## The Columbo Method

Named after the TV detective who solved cases by appearing bumbling and non-threatening.

**The approach**: Adopt an "antipotent" persona—non-authoritative, unassuming, even slightly confused. This lowers their perceived threat level and causes them to drop their guard.

**How it works**:
1. Start casual and disarming: "I'm probably missing something obvious here..."
2. Ask simple, almost naive questions that prompt explanation
3. Let them feel like they're educating you
4. Slip in the real question once they're relaxed

**Example phrases**:
- "I'm confused about something—help me understand..."
- "This might be a dumb question, but..."
- "I'm sure there's a good reason, but why..."
- "Bear with me, I'm still wrapping my head around this..."

**The "one more thing"**: In conversation, after they think you've moved on, circle back to the unresolved question. The mental checkout makes the answer more honest.

**When to use**: When someone is giving polished, rehearsed, or evasive answers. The Columbo approach disarms the "media training."

## Labeling Emotions

Explicitly naming the emotion you perceive, which demonstrates understanding and often prompts deeper disclosure.

**The approach**: State what you observe about their emotional state, framed tentatively.

**Example phrases**:
- "It sounds like that was frustrating"
- "I'm sensing some hesitation when you talk about X"
- "That seems to have been a difficult moment"
- "There's something in how you describe Y—like it still bothers you"

**Why it works**:
- Demonstrates you're actually listening
- Gives them permission to acknowledge the emotion
- Often unlocks the story behind the feeling
- Builds trust through demonstrated empathy

**When to use**: When decisions have emotional weight (career changes, relationships, past failures), or when someone is being rational about something that clearly has emotional charge.

## The Pratfall Effect

Showing a small imperfection to become more relatable and human.

**The approach**: Admit to a minor flaw, confusion, or mistake. This triggers reciprocity—they become more willing to share their own imperfections.

**Example phrases**:
- "I completely misread that situation when I first heard about it"
- "I have to admit, I don't fully understand X"
- "I made the same mistake once and it taught me..."

**Why it works**: Perfection creates distance. Small imperfections signal "we're equals, you can be honest."

**When to use**: When someone seems to be performing or impression-managing rather than being candid. Your vulnerability invites theirs.

## Behavioral Questions

Ask about specific past actions rather than hypothetical future intentions.

**The principle**: Past behavior predicts future behavior better than stated intentions. "What would you do if..." invites idealized answers. "What did you do when..." reveals reality.

**Structure**: "Tell me about a specific time when [situation]. Walk me through what happened."

**Examples**:
- Instead of: "How do you handle conflict?"
- Ask: "Tell me about the last significant conflict you had at work. What happened?"

- Instead of: "Would you take a pay cut for more interesting work?"
- Ask: "Have you ever turned down more money for something else? What was the situation?"

**Follow-ups for behavioral questions**:
- "What were you thinking at that moment?"
- "What did you do next?"
- "Looking back, what would you do differently?"
- "What did that teach you?"

**When to use**: Whenever you need to understand what someone actually does (vs. what they say they'd do). Especially useful for understanding values, decision-making patterns, and reactions under pressure.

## Perspective-Taking Prompts

Help someone see the situation from another viewpoint.

**Example phrases**:
- "How would [other stakeholder] describe this situation?"
- "If your [future self / past self / mentor] looked at this, what would they see?"
- "What would someone who disagreed with you say?"
- "If you were advising someone else in this situation, what would you tell them?"

**Why it works**: We're often trapped in our own perspective. Explicitly shifting viewpoint reveals blind spots and unexamined assumptions.

**When to use**: When someone seems stuck, when there's an obvious blind spot, or when you want to surface considerations they haven't voiced.

## The "What Else" Technique

After they've answered, ask "what else?" and wait.

**How it works**:
1. They give an answer
2. You say: "What else?"
3. They give a second answer (often the real one)
4. You say: "And what else?"
5. The third answer is frequently the most revealing

**Why it works**: First answers are often rehearsed or socially acceptable. "What else?" signals you want more than the surface, without directing where to go.

**When to use**: After any answer that feels complete but shallow. Also useful when exploring options, concerns, or motivations.

## Contrarian Probe

Gently challenge their stated position to see how deep it goes.

**Example phrases**:
- "What if the opposite were true?"
- "Play devil's advocate with me—what's the case against this?"
- "Someone might argue that X. How would you respond?"
- "Is there a scenario where this would be the wrong choice?"

**Why it works**: Forces articulation of underlying reasoning. If they can't defend it, the position may be shallow or inherited.

**When to use**: When a decision seems too quick or a belief seems unexamined. Not to argue, but to test the foundation.

---

## Scaling Questions

Quantify subjective states to make them discussable.

**Structure**: "On a scale of 1-10, where [1 = X] and [10 = Y], where are you?"

**Follow-ups**:
- "What would move you from a 6 to an 8?"
- "What's keeping you from being a 3?"
- "What would it take to be a 10?"

**Why it works**: Forces precision on fuzzy feelings. The follow-ups reveal what actually matters and what's blocking.

**When to use**: When dealing with confidence levels, satisfaction, certainty, commitment. Makes the intangible tangible.

---

## Combining Tactics

These tactics compound. Example sequence:

1. **Columbo** to disarm: "I'm probably missing something, but..."
2. **Behavioral question** to get real data: "Tell me about a time when..."
3. **Label the emotion** that surfaces: "That sounds like it was difficult"
4. **What else** to go deeper: "What else was going on?"
5. **Contrarian probe** to test the conclusion: "What if you had stayed?"
6. **Perspective-taking** to reveal blind spots: "How would they describe what happened?"

Don't use all tactics in every interview. Select based on what the conversation needs.
