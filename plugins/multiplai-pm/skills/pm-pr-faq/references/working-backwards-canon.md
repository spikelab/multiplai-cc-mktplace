# Working Backwards — Canon

## Origin

The Working Backwards process originated at Amazon in the early 2000s. Canonized by Colin Bryar and Bill Carr in *Working Backwards* (2021) — both were senior leaders at Amazon during the formative period (Bryar as Bezos's Chief of Staff / shadow advisor, Carr as VP Digital Media).

Working Backwards inverts the standard product process. Standard process: have an idea → build it → write the launch announcement. Working Backwards: write the launch announcement first → if it's not compelling, fix or kill the idea → only then build.

The core insight: **the press release forces clarity that solution-thinking doesn't**. If you can't write a credible PR before building, you don't actually know what the customer gets, who they are, or why they'd care. Building anyway is gambling.

## The Two Artifacts

### Artifact 1 — The Press Release (PR)

One page. Dated at the future launch date. Reads like an actual press release. Constraints:

- **Headline.** States the benefit, not the feature. "Customers Can Now [Outcome]" beats "Acme Launches [Feature Name]."
- **Subhead.** Names the persona and the outcome in one sentence.
- **Summary paragraph.** 3-5 sentences. If everything else were cut, this would still convey the announcement.
- **Problem paragraph.** What was broken before, from the customer's POV. Specific.
- **Solution paragraph.** How the new thing works for the customer. Capabilities, not platform abstractions.
- **Customer quote.** Fictional but plausible. Names a specific outcome. In the persona's voice.
- **Internal leader quote.** Real leader name (the user's leader, or the user). States strategic intent without marketing-speak.
- **Getting started.** How a customer adopts this. Concrete. 24-48 hour experience.

The PR is the artifact that goes to "the customer" (treated as a real audience even though no customer reads it). It must be credible to a journalist who has read a thousand product launches.

### Artifact 2 — The Internal FAQ

10-15 questions, each with an honest answer. Three flavors of questions:

1. **Customer-facing questions** — what a customer or journalist might ask after reading the PR.
2. **Internal questions** — what the team needs to align on (build, business model, persona prioritization, unit economics).
3. **Adversarial questions** — what a skeptic, competitor, or senior leader would ask to dismiss the initiative.

The FAQ is where the work actually happens. The PR shows the destination; the FAQ shows whether the path is real.

## The Underlying Principles

### Customer-obsessed, working backwards

Bezos's framing: **start from the customer experience and work backwards to the technology**. The PR forces you to articulate the customer experience first. Only after that experience is convincing do you ask "what would we need to build?" Most products fail because they reverse the order.

### Two-pizza teams, one-page documents

The Working Backwards artifacts are designed to be **read silently in a meeting** by attendees who haven't seen them before. This is why the PR is 1 page (the room can read it in 5 minutes) and the FAQ is 10-15 questions (enough to surface issues, not so many that the meeting stalls).

### Documents over decks

Amazon famously banned PowerPoint in senior meetings in favor of 6-pagers — and the PR-FAQ is the parent form. The argument: bullets compress thinking; prose forces complete thoughts. If you can write the PR-FAQ, you can defend it; if you can only PowerPoint it, you're hiding gaps.

### Working Backwards as a kill mechanism

The most underrated function of the PR-FAQ is to *kill bad ideas before they're built*. The discipline: if you can't write a compelling PR, the idea isn't ready. This is uncomfortable because teams emotionally commit before writing PRs. But killing a bad idea at the PR stage is 100x cheaper than killing it after a quarter of engineering work.

## What a Good PR Looks Like

Length: 1 page. Tone: factual, customer-centric, no buzzwords. Structure: as above. Voice: as if a real journalist were rewriting an actual press release for clarity.

### Good PR markers

- The headline names a customer outcome with a verb of change.
- The subhead identifies the persona by recognizable behavior, not demographics.
- The problem paragraph references a *specific* failure mode of the prior state — not "things were hard."
- The solution paragraph describes *what the customer does*, not what the platform provides.
- The customer quote uses customer-vocabulary, not product-marketing-vocabulary.
- A competent customer support lead, reading this PR cold, can answer "what does this do" in one sentence.

### Bad PR markers (the smell tests)

- The headline mentions a technology before a benefit ("AI-Powered Workflow Automation Now Available").
- The subhead doesn't identify a persona.
- The problem paragraph could apply to any company in any industry ("In today's fast-paced market...").
- The solution paragraph mostly describes the company's capabilities ("Acme's industry-leading platform...").
- The customer quote sounds like a press release writer wrote it ("...transformative impact across our organization").
- Three buzzwords in the first paragraph.

## What a Good FAQ Looks Like

Length: 10-15 questions. Each Q has a 2-5 sentence answer. Tone: candid; internal-only. Posture: includes things the team would rather not address.

### Good FAQ markers

- At least one question admits an explicit limitation.
- At least one question names a real tradeoff (something cut, deferred, or not built).
- At least one question states the riskiest assumption (the thing that, if false, breaks the initiative).
- Answers like "we don't know yet, but we'll learn by [date] via [method]" appear.
- The FAQ would be embarrassing if a competitor read it — because it's that honest.

### Bad FAQ markers

- All questions are self-flattering ("How is Acme positioned to lead this space?").
- All answers are confident ("We expect strong adoption from day one").
- No question names a tradeoff.
- No question names a risk.
- The FAQ would be perfectly acceptable to publish as a marketing blog post.

## Variant: The 6-Pager

The 6-pager is the Amazon long-form version of the PR-FAQ — typically used for larger strategic decisions. Structure:

1. PR (1 page)
2. FAQ (1-2 pages)
3. Customer experience descriptions / mock screenshots (1-2 pages)
4. Visual / data tables (as needed)
5. Detailed financials, unit economics, or methodology (1-2 pages)
6. Appendix / open questions

The 1-page PR + FAQ format covered by this skill is the right artifact when:
- The initiative is at the "should we build this" stage
- The audience needs to make a decision quickly
- Heavy detail would obscure the core question

The 6-pager is the right form when:
- The initiative is large and approval requires detailed proof
- Multiple stakeholders need to engage with the financials, customer experience, or methodology
- The PR-FAQ format has already been workshopped and locked

For 6-pager work, use this skill to draft the PR-FAQ portion, then extend.
